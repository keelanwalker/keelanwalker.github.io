My Learning Agreement

14 November 2010

As pointed out in my last blog, the result of my Myers Briggs personality test returned that I was a DEBATER PERSONALITY (ENTP, -A/-T).

I found it an interesting and astonishingly accurate assessment. There are things about my personality that are identified in this analysis which I would love to reject, but, the reality is that they're quite true.

A very basic summary of my personality type is..

We are smart and curious thinkers who love solving new and challenging problems. We are bored by routine, outspoken and are relentless debaters.

I think there are a number of good attributes identified in the full summary of my personality assessment, which would not only make me a valuable team member, but also a good developer. The tough part will be managing the attributes that would also make me a terrible team member.

I have decided to identify the 3 main things that I will need to be mindful of in order to work well with the rest of my learning team

    These are:
  • limiting the urge to argue the insignificant details.
  • maintaining a respectful level of sensitivity about other peoples feelings.
  • Exercising tolerance

    • I chose these 3 things because to me, they appear to be the worst character attributes that are identified in the test. Therefore, it makes sense that I would address them first and foremost

      My plan to manage my workload, also based on my character attributes would be to utilise the following approaches:
  • Remain focused on achieving short term, small, incremental goals.
  • Identifying the signs and signals of procrastination and avoiding it.
  • Getting enough, or a healthy amount of sleep. (Huge!)

    • I’m naturally inclined to try and solve most problems myself, however, the pitfall to this tactic is committing too much time to finding a solution which could result in missing deadlines. Therefore, to prevent this, my plan is to read the sprint overview at the beginning of each sprint and scheduling a study plan based on the suggested time frames. As I work through each exercise, any confusing topics or areas can be temporarily sidelined and revisited at the end of the exercise. If I am still having trouble solving the issue, then seek help, rather than leaving it to the end.

    On the occasion that I may need help with a particular sprint, I would hope to expect a turn around in answer of less than half a day. I believe half the battle of communicating an issue is describing or articulating my problem so it makes sense to the facilitator, in order to receive a clear answer.

    During bootcamp, I will have no other known or planned commitments inside of regular study hours.  Going in to boot camp my commitments to studying is a minimum of 4 hours per day (2 hours in morning and 2 hours in the afternoon), 5 days a week.
